# Foodie Diary
### *The Solitary Gourmet*

---

There is a particular freedom that comes with eating alone.

Most people would not describe it that way. They imagine the solo diner as a figure of pathos — the man at the corner table with his nose in a book, the woman nursing a glass of wine while couples laugh around her, the traveler who orders quickly and eats faster, eager to dissolve back into anonymity. Loneliness made visible. An absence given a table for one.

But they are wrong. They have confused solitude with loneliness, and the distinction is everything.

I discovered this difference by accident, the way most important discoveries are made. It was a Tuesday in early autumn, some years ago now, in a city I had come to for work and found myself stranded in by a canceled meeting. I had three hours to fill and no one to fill them with. On a narrow side street, I found a small restaurant — eight tables, perhaps, a handwritten menu taped to the window, the smell of dashi and charcoal drifting under the door. I went in. I sat alone. I ordered without consulting anyone. And somewhere between the first sip of cold barley tea and the final, quiet scrape of my bowl, something loosened in me that I had not known was tight.

I have been eating alone, deliberately and joyfully, ever since.

---

## The Education of a Solitary Eater

To eat alone well is a skill, and like all skills, it must be learned.

The first lesson is attention. When you dine with others, conversation fills the space that food occupies. You talk, you laugh, you listen, and the meal becomes a backdrop — pleasant, perhaps even delicious, but secondary. The real event is the social occasion. The food is the setting, not the story.

Alone, the food becomes the story.

I remember sitting at a counter in a tiny ramen shop in a basement I nearly walked past. The chef was a compact, silent man in his sixties who moved with the economy of someone who had performed the same actions ten thousand times and found no reason to alter any of them. He did not look up when I sat down. He placed a glass of water before me without being asked. I ordered the house ramen — the only option, it turned out — and waited.

The broth arrived first, as it should. It was a tonkotsu, the color of old ivory, with a surface shimmer of fat that caught the light above the counter. I lifted the bowl and smelled it before I tasted it. This is something you can do when you eat alone. You can smell your food without embarrassment. You can take your time. You can close your eyes.

The broth tasted of twenty hours. Of pork bones split and roasted and simmered until they surrendered everything they had. There was depth beneath the depth — a faint sweetness, a mineral bitterness at the very back of the throat, something almost floral that I could not name. I sat with that broth for a long moment, trying to understand it, the way you sit with a piece of music that is doing something unexpected.

This is the education. You learn to slow down. You learn that eating is not refueling — it is inquiry.

## A Map of Hunger

My diary, if you were to read it, would look like a strange kind of atlas.

There are entries from cities I know well and cities I passed through only once. There are notes on a hand-pulled noodle shop in a market where no one spoke a language I shared, where I ordered by pointing and ate standing up because there were no seats. There are paragraphs devoted to a single oyster eaten on a dock at five in the afternoon, still cold from the sea, dressed with nothing but its own liquor and a squeeze of lemon from a vendor who seemed to find my enthusiasm disproportionate to the occasion.

I have written about a plate of pasta eaten in a village restaurant where the owner — a woman in her seventies with the look of someone who has survived interesting times — brought me a second portion without asking and refused any additional payment. I have written about a breakfast of rice congee eaten alone in a hotel dining room while rain smeared the windows and the city outside went about its morning, indifferent to me, and I to it. I was happy.

The map that emerges is not of places, exactly. It is of states of mind. Of the particular quality of hunger that makes food taste like revelation. Of the equally particular satisfaction of having nowhere to be afterward — of being able to order a second coffee and remain at the table while the lunch rush clears and the restaurant settles into its afternoon quiet, and no one needs anything from you.

The solitary gourmet learns to read restaurants the way a naturalist reads landscapes. You notice things. The way a good server reads the room without hovering. The way a kitchen communicates its confidence through the simplicity of its menu. The relationship between a chef's ambition and a dining room's intimacy — the places that try too hard to impress and the places that have long since stopped caring what you think and are simply cooking.

I prefer the latter, almost always.

## On the Question of the Counter

If there is a theology to solitary dining, the counter is its altar.

Not every restaurant has one, and not every counter is worth sitting at. But when the configuration is right — when the counter faces an open kitchen and the kitchen is doing real work — there is no better seat in the house. You are inside the performance and outside it simultaneously. You watch without being watched. You receive without having to explain yourself.

I once spent an entire evening at the counter of a small French restaurant run by a chef who had trained in Paris and then, for reasons he declined to explain, opened a twelve-seat restaurant in a city that did not particularly expect him. He cooked alone. One dish at a time, composed with a precision that was almost aggressive in its calm. A terrine of vegetables, each layer distinct in color and texture, sliced with a knife that moved through it like certainty. A fish, simply roasted, with a sauce built from its own bones that made the fish more fully itself.

I did not speak to him much. He did not invite conversation and I did not offer it. But at some point during the meal, as he was plating something with the focused expression of a man doing the only thing he has ever wanted to do, he looked up briefly and said, in the tone of someone noting weather: "The turbot tonight is exceptional. I wasn't sure until I cooked it."

He returned to his work. I thought about that sentence for the rest of the meal. The honesty of it. The acknowledgment that cooking is not a demonstration but an act of discovery — that even the cook doesn't know, until the moment of cooking, what the food will become.

That is what I mean when I say the counter is the best seat in the house. Not for the view, exactly. For the possibility of moments like that one.

## What the Solitary Gourmet Carries

My kit is small.

A notebook — always, without fail. A pen that works reliably in the cold. A paperback, for restaurants where staring into the middle distance would be interpreted as distress. An appetite that I have learned to manage carefully: not so hungry that the first thing on the table tastes miraculous regardless of its quality, and not so full that the nuances are lost. There is a sweet spot, a particular calibration of want, that makes eating a pleasure rather than a compulsion or a chore.

I write in the notebook while I eat, which some people find strange. The notes are fragmentary — single words, sometimes, or a phrase that tries to catch something the tongue knows but the mind hasn't translated yet. *Smoke and salt. Sweetness that doesn't stay. Something like a green smell, cut grass, a garden after rain.* Later, at the hotel or on the train, I expand these fragments into something resembling prose.

The notebook is not, primarily, a record. It is an instrument of attention. Writing sharpens perception the way a lens sharpens light. The act of trying to describe a flavor forces you to actually taste it — to move past the simple register of *good* or *not good* into the more interesting territory of *what is this, precisely, and why does it matter to me?*

This is the solitary gourmet's real discipline. Not the accumulation of restaurants visited or dishes tasted. Not the cultivation of authority or expertise. It is this: the practice of paying attention to what you eat, and taking that attention seriously enough to commit it to paper. To say: this moment, this bowl, this table, this particular afternoon light — these things were real, and they were mine, and I was present for them.

## Difficult Meals

I should not pretend that solitary dining is always a pleasure.

There are restaurants that make you feel your aloneness as an accusation. Places designed for celebration, for announcement, for the performance of occasion — the grand brasseries with their theatrical service, the special-event restaurants where every table is a couple marking a milestone. In these places, the single diner becomes a kind of category error. The staff are kind, perhaps, but their kindness has the quality of accommodation rather than welcome. The table for one is the table that wasn't meant to be there.

I have learned to identify these places before entering them and to walk past with equanimity.

But there are also meals that are difficult in a different way — meals that move you unexpectedly, that open something you weren't prepared for. I ate alone once in a small restaurant in a city where I had lived for a year, a decade earlier. The restaurant had not existed when I lived there, but it occupied a building I had walked past almost every day. I ordered something simple — a roast chicken, bread, a carafe of local wine — and somewhere in the middle of the meal, with no particular warning, I found myself thinking about who I had been in that year and who I had become since. The food was fine. The evening was not about the food.

This, too, is part of solitary dining. The absence of social obligation creates a kind of interior space, and sometimes what fills that space is not contemplation of the food but something older and less comfortable. The meal becomes a container for whatever is actually on your mind.

I have learned not to resist this. The best meals are not always the most comfortable ones. Sometimes the most honest thing a meal can do is to stop being about itself.

## A Day in the Life

Let me try to describe a recent day, since abstraction is the enemy of appetite.

I was in a port city for a conference that required my presence in a conference hall for seven hours and my presence nowhere else. The morning was my own.

I woke early and walked to a market that opened at five for the fishermen and the restaurant buyers and had, by the time I arrived at half past six, already transacted most of its serious business. The stalls were still full, however, and I moved through them slowly, looking at everything. Squid of varying sizes laid out on crushed ice. Sea urchin in their spiky shells, waiting to be opened. Fish I couldn't name arranged by color as much as species.

I bought nothing — I had no kitchen — but I looked for a long time.

Breakfast was at a counter near the market entrance: a bowl of clam soup and two fried eggs on rice, with a small dish of pickled vegetables and a cup of green tea that had been brewed with the casual confidence of someone who has made green tea ten thousand times and is no longer interested in praise for it. The soup was bright with salt and the sweetness of the clams and something deeper underneath — the sea itself, distilled. I ate slowly. I wrote three lines in my notebook. *Clam soup, market breakfast. The sea at the bottom of everything. Tea without ceremony.*

Lunch was a problem of abundance: too many options, too little time before the conference resumed. I chose by walking until something felt right — a noodle shop, standing room only, where a teenager served me a bowl of cold sesame noodles with a practiced efficiency that suggested she had better things to do and was right. The noodles were perfect. Cold and silky, with a sauce that was rich without being heavy, and a scattering of cucumber and sesame that provided texture and relief. I ate standing at a shelf along the wall, reading a placard about the history of the neighborhood that may or may not have been accurate.

Dinner was more deliberate. I had asked at the hotel — not the concierge, who would have recommended somewhere expensive and internationally legible, but the woman at the front desk who had grown up in the city and still lived twenty minutes away. She named a place without hesitation, wrote the address on a piece of paper, and added: "Tell them you want the beef, even if it's not on the menu. They always have it."

The restaurant was on a street I would not have found on my own — small, unmarked except for a noren curtain in the doorway, eight seats at a counter and four small tables. The woman who seated me was older, with the manner of someone who has been running the same room for thirty years and has no patience for uncertainty. I told her about the beef.

She looked at me for a moment — assessing, I think, whether I could be trusted with it — and then nodded and left.

What arrived was a small clay pot of braised short rib in a broth that was dark and sweet and complex, served with a bowl of white rice and a dish of pickled daikon that cut through the richness with bright, acidic authority. I ate it slowly, the way the dish demanded. Between bites I sipped cold barley tea. The restaurant around me went about its evening — other diners, quiet conversations, the sound of cooking from somewhere behind a curtain.

I wrote in my notebook afterward, on the train back to the hotel: *The beef. The thing she said with her eyes before she brought it. The way the rice was perfect without being noticed. Thirty years in one room. This is what it looks like when someone knows what they're doing.*

## Why It Matters

I am sometimes asked to justify the solitary gourmet's project. Why eat alone? Why write about it? Why elevate something as ordinary as a meal into the subject of sustained, serious attention?

The honest answer is: because the alternative is not paying attention, and not paying attention is a kind of waste.

We spend a significant portion of our lives eating. Most of that time passes without leaving a trace. We eat at our desks, in our cars, standing over the kitchen sink. We eat while looking at our phones. We eat the same things we ate last week because thinking about food requires more imagination than we currently have. The meals dissolve into one another and become simply the background noise of having a body.

The solitary gourmet pushes back against this. Not through exclusivity or expense or the accumulation of gastronomic credentials. But through attention. Through the willingness to sit down, alone, with a plate of food and take it seriously. To ask: what is this? Where did it come from? Who made it, and what were they thinking? What does it taste like, precisely? What does it remind me of, and why?

These are not difficult questions. They require no expertise and no special palate. They require only the willingness to slow down and pay attention — which is, I think, the basic requirement of any life lived with some degree of consciousness.

The food is almost beside the point. Almost. The food is the occasion for attention, the invitation to be present in the way we forget to be present. A good bowl of soup won't save you. But it might, for twenty minutes, at a small table in an unremarkable restaurant in a city where no one knows your name, return you to yourself.

That is enough. That is, in fact, quite a lot.

---

*The solitary gourmet is always alone, but never lonely. There is too much to eat, and too much to notice, for loneliness to find a foothold.*
